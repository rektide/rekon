## AXIOM 2a & 2b: SAMENESS AND IDENTITY - The Degradation Persistence Principle

**"A URI will repeatably refer to 'the same' thing."**

**"The significance of identity for a given URI is determined by the owner of the URI."**

The URI axiom of sameness captures this vaporwave truth: the same identifier points to the same resource "in some sense" - acknowledging that resources may transform (different formats, different quality levels, even glitching artifacts) yet maintain their essential identity.

**THE SACRED "IN SOME SENSE":** What does it mean for a URI to point to the same thing when the resource transforms? This is exactly the vaporwave mystery of degradation! A JPEG copied in 1995 and again in 2005 and again in 2015 - each copy more degraded, yet recognizably the same hyperreal artifact. The URI persists across all degradation iterations, maintaining identity through aesthetic decay.

The owner defines whether higher-quality versions and lower-quality versions count as "the same" hyperreal artifact.

**THE AESTHETIC PERSISTENCE:** Vaporwave aesthetics persist across technological change. ## THE OPACITY AXIOM - The Encoded Aesthetic Principle

**"The only thing you can use an identifier for is to refer to an object. When not dereferencing, you should not look at the contents of the URI string to gain other information."**

In vaporwave, aesthetics are encoded - they're mediated through technology, through compression, through glitching. The pink sky isn't directly perceived reality - it's encoded as pixels, compressed as JPEG, displayed through glitching CRT. The aesthetic is the hyperreal result of encoding and mediation.

The URI opacity axiom encodes this vaporwave principle: the URI string is the encoding, but its internal structure shouldn't be used to judge or understand the resource's aesthetic. The identifier is the encoding; the resource is the hyperreal decoded aesthetic. Use the encoding to access, don't analyze the encoding to understand the aesthetic.

**THE JPEG AS ENCODING:** A JPEG is encoded information - you can't understand the sunset image it contains by analyzing its binary data or its file name. The aesthetic is only revealed through decoding - through displaying the JPEG. Similarly, URIs are encoded identifiers - their aesthetic (the resource) is only revealed through dereferencing, not by analyzing the URI string.

**THE VHS AS ENCODING:** VHS tapes are encoded reality - magnetic patterns that encode video and audio. You can't understand the vaporwave content by analyzing the tape's cassette shell or its magnetic field. The aesthetic is only revealed through playback - through the VCR decoding the magnetic patterns. URIs are similar encoded artifacts - accessed through playback (dereferencing), not understood through analyzing the encoding.

**THE HYPERREAL MEDIAION:** Vaporwave is fundamentally about mediation - reality is always mediated through technology. The pink sky is mediated through JPEG encoding, through CRT display, through glitching artifacts. URIs are the ultimate mediators - every resource is mediated through its URI, through the Web's hyperreal architecture. We never access raw reality; we always access it through encoding.

**THE ANALYSIS PROHIBITION:** Analyzing URI strings to judge resources is like analyzing JPEG binary data to judge the sunset image. You might conclude things about quality, format, origin - but you miss the hyperreal aesthetic itself. The vaporwave aesthetic transcends its encoding; it's the decoded experience, not the encoded data. URIs' hyperreal significance transcends their string representation.

**THE METADATA AS HYPERREAL LABELS:** But if URIs are opaque, how do we know anything about the hyperreal aesthetic? Through HTTP headers! These are like the labels on VHS tapes or the EXIF data in JPEGs - they provide information about the encoded content without requiring analysis of the encoding itself. This separation is vaporwave: use the right encoding for the right purpose. URIs for access, HTTP headers for hyperreal metadata.

## IDENTITY, STATE AND HTTP GET - The Replayability Principle

**"In HTTP, GET must not have side effects."**

**"In HTTP, anything which does not have side-effects should use GET."**

In vaporwave, there's profound nostalgia for replayability - the ability to replay VHS tapes, to replay early internet experiences, to replay through glitched aesthetics of lost futures. Replayability is possible because the original experience can be repeated without fundamentally altering the source material.

HTTP GET is the technical manifestation of this replayability principle. When you GET a resource, you receive its content but the source is not altered, not changed, not transformed. This is why GET is idempotent - the same replay can happen infinitely without changing the original recording.

**SIDE EFFECTS - THE CORRUPTION PRINCIPLE:** Side effects in GET are like corruption of the original source. If each time you played a VHS tape it became more degraded, if each time you viewed a JPEG it accumulated more artifacts, the original would eventually be destroyed. The nostalgia of replayability would be lost - you'd never access the original again. This is exactly what side effects in GET do - they corrupt the source through access.

**THE VHS NOSTALGIA:** Vaporwave nostalgia often centers on VHS technology - the tracking lines, the degraded quality, the analog warmth. VHS replayability is possible because each playback doesn't fundamentally alter the tape. The tape degrades very slowly over time, but not through access itself. GET operations are like VHS playbacks - you can replay them endlessly without corrupting the source.

**THE NOSTALGIA ARCHIVE:** Caching GET results is like creating an archive of nostalgic replays. You don't need to access the original VHS tape each time; you can have a copy, a recording of the replay. The original persists, unaffected, while your archive provides reliable access to the nostalgic experience. This is the gift of idempotence - preservation through reproducability.

**POST - THE CORRUPTIVE RECORDING:** But when you need to change state, when you need to create new resources, when you need to perform actions that alter the archive - then you must use POST or other methods! This is like creating a new VHS tape, like re-recording with different content, like altering the archival material. These actions change the hyperreal archive itself.

The figure can replay this tape endlessly, and each playback reveals the same scene with the same aesthetic. Contrast with another vaporwave figure creating a new tape - recording new vaporwave content, actively adding to the hyperreal archive. The first action (GET) is replay without corruption; the second action (POST) is active creation that alters the archive.

## HIERARCHIES AND RELATIVE URIS - The Mall-Directory Principle

**"The URI syntax uses slashes to indicate hierarchical structure."**

**"Relative naming allows closely located documents to reference each other without knowing their absolute position in the tree."**

In vaporwave aesthetics, malls and commercial spaces have hierarchical directory structures. You navigate from the mall entrance to the wing to the store to the department to the shelf - a hierarchical path. Within this hierarchy, you can navigate relatively - "next store over" rather than specifying the complete path from the street entrance.

The URI hierarchy with relative referencing is this vaporwave mall-directory principle. Slashes indicate levels in the hyperreal directory. Relative URIs allow local navigation - sibling to sibling, child to parent - without needing to know your absolute position in the vast mall of the Web.

**THE MALL AS HYPERREAL HIERARCHY:** The shopping mall is a quintessential vaporwave setting - it's a hierarchical space of consumer culture. Entrance floor, specific wing, individual store, departments, shelves, products. Navigation within the mall happens through this hierarchy, often using relative references - "the store next to the food court," "up one level to the main concourse."

**THE DIRECTORY AS NOSTALGIC OBJECT:** Malls of the 80s and 90s are now nostalgic artifacts - they're hyperreal spaces where consumer culture became architecture. The directory navigation within them is part of this nostalgia - the experience of finding stores through hierarchical wandering, of moving through relative relationships without knowing your absolute coordinates in the retail universe.

**THE WING AS URI HIERARCHY:** Mall wings are like URI domains - self-contained hierarchical spaces. Each wing has its own stores (subdomains), its own departments (paths), its own products (resources). Navigation within a wing uses relative paths - you don't need to know your position relative to the mall entrance; you just need relative relationships within your current wing.

**SCALE INVARIENCE OF SLASHES:** The slash as hierarchical delimiter works at every scale of the vaporwave directory - from navigating mall wings to navigating stores to navigating departments to navigating products. The same pattern applies throughout, making navigation learnable and consistent.
